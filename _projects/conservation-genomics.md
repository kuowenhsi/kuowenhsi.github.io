I use conservation genomics to diagnose the processes that limit rare plant persistence and to translate those results into management decisions. This work combines population-scale sampling, whole-genome sequencing, field observations, and common-garden experiments to estimate genetic diversity, population structure, demographic history, connectivity, genetic load, and adaptive potential.

My current postdoctoral research at the Missouri Botanical Garden focuses on plants in river-floodplain ecosystems, where recurrent disturbance, local extirpation, recolonization, and directional connectivity shape metapopulation dynamics. In the federally endangered *Boltonia decurrens*, I am using whole-genome data and common-garden experiments to identify diversity hotspots, major breaks in connectivity, and traits associated with local adaptation.

A broader goal of this research program is species-aware conservation: defining conservation units, assessing whether assisted gene flow or genetic rescue may be warranted, and designing in situ and ex situ strategies that retain evolutionary potential. I am especially interested in applying these approaches to endangered plants across contrasting habitats, including floodplains, limestone bluffs, calcareous glades, and prairie systems.
