I study how selection, gene flow, drift, and demographic history shape plant distributions, trait variation, and local adaptation. Much of my work asks how common and widespread species persist across environmental gradients and how their responses to climate change may affect broader ecological communities.

During my Ph.D. at Washington University in St. Louis, I used white clover (*Trifolium repens*) as a model for understanding local adaptation and gene regulation in a polyploid genome. This work connected climate-associated genomic divergence to life-history variation, drought response, copy-number variation in the cyanogenesis defense pathway, and the genetic basis of a leaf-mark polymorphism.

Looking forward, I am interested in using steep elevational gradients as natural laboratories for studying adaptive trait divergence. Native species with broad elevational distributions, paired common gardens, quantitative phenotyping, GWAS, and landscape genomics can reveal how adaptive variation is maintained despite connectivity and how lowland and montane populations may respond to future climates.
