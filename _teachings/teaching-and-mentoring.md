My teaching philosophy centers on four commitments. First, active learning helps students understand how scientific claims are generated and validated, not simply what conclusions appear in textbooks. Second, cooperative learning gives students practice explaining ideas, revising arguments, and learning from peers. Third, scientific thinking should be practiced directly through hypothesis building, data interpretation, and evidence-based reasoning. Finally, inclusive teaching requires attention to differences in background, access, and prior training so that students can participate fully.

I have taught evolutionary biology, general biology, and botany lab as a discussion section instructor, lab instructor, and teaching assistant. These experiences included guiding paper discussions, supporting large lecture courses, designing lab activities under practical constraints, holding office hours, leading review sessions, and grading written work.

My mentoring style changes with student stage. For undergraduates, I emphasize exploration, research literacy, teamwork, time management, and communication. For master's students, I focus on developing feasible thesis projects and building professional research skills. For doctoral students, I aim to foster independence, originality, and ownership of the full research process, from proposal development through publication.

To date, I have mentored 17 undergraduate and graduate researchers in Taiwan and the United States. Several mentees have contributed to peer-reviewed publications, one undergraduate mentee won a competitive research grant, and two graduate mentees completed M.S. degrees at National Taiwan University.
