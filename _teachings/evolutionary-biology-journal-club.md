This course is designed as a small-cohort journal club that helps students read primary literature like researchers. Each session focuses on the central question, hypotheses, assumptions, data quality, alternative explanations, and broader value of the paper.

Students take turns facilitating discussion rather than delivering formal presentations. The goal is to build confidence in scientific discussion, practice respectful critique, and connect specialized thesis work to broader concepts in evolutionary biology.

| Week | Theme |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Variation in natural populations |
| 2 | Mutation and molecular evolution |
| 3 | Hardy-Weinberg principle and mating systems |
| 4 | Genetic drift and effective population size |
| 5 | Population structure and gene flow |
| 6 | Recombination, linkage disequilibrium, and genomic architecture |
| 7 | Selection: fitness, modes of selection, and inference |
| 8 | Local adaptation and genotype-by-environment variation |
| 9 | Quantitative genetics |
| 10 | Life-history evolution and trade-offs |
| 11 | Sexual selection, conflict, and coevolution |
| 12 | Social evolution and kin selection |
| 13 | Speciation, hybrid zones, and introgression |
| 14 | Phylogenetics, comparative methods, and macroevolution |
